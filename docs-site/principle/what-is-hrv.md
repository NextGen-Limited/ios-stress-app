# What is HRV?

HRV (Heart Rate Variability) measures the natural variation in time intervals between consecutive heartbeats. Unlike heart rate (beats per minute), HRV captures the subtle irregularity between beats — a sign of a healthy, adaptive autonomic nervous system.

## How the Autonomic Nervous System Controls HRV

Your autonomic nervous system has two branches:

- **Sympathetic nervous system** — activates stress responses, accelerates heart rate
- **Parasympathetic nervous system** — promotes rest and digestion, slows heart rate

These two systems balance each other. When you're relaxed, the parasympathetic system introduces natural beat-to-beat variation — **high HRV**. Under stress, the sympathetic system dominates and reduces that variation — **low HRV**.

| HRV State | What It Indicates |
|-----------|-------------------|
| High HRV | Better autonomic balance, greater adaptability, lower physiological stress |
| Low HRV | Stress, fatigue, or health strain |

## HRV and Stress: The Research Link

Research has found links between lower HRV and higher stress levels, as well as mental health conditions such as anxiety and depression. Long-term exposure to high-stress environments can lead to autonomic nervous system imbalance and persistently lower HRV.

Under chronic stress, the sympathetic nervous system becomes overactivated while the parasympathetic nervous system is suppressed — reducing HRV and increasing cardiovascular risk.

## Important Factors That Influence HRV

HRV is affected by age, gender, physical fitness, lifestyle, and genetics. Never compare your absolute HRV number against another person's — compare against your own personal baseline.
