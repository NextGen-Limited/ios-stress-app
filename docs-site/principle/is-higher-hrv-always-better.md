# Is Higher HRV Always Better?

## Understanding High and Low HRV

**Generally:** higher HRV correlates with better cardiovascular health, lower stress, and superior recovery from physical exertion. Conversely, lower HRV suggests the opposite.

However, there are important nuances:

1. **Compare against yourself, not others.** HRV varies significantly between individuals and shouldn't be judged solely by its absolute value. StressMonitor evaluates each reading relative to your personal 30-day history.

2. **Sudden spikes can be a warning sign.** If your HRV jumps significantly above your typical range for one or several days, it may indicate accumulated stress — intensive workouts, a minor infection, or abrupt shifts in activity and sleep patterns. See [What is Undefined?](./about-undefined) for more on abnormally high readings.

3. **Chasing high HRV is counterproductive.** Artificially forcing HRV higher through unsustainable lifestyle changes is not the goal. Gradual improvement within your personal range represents optimal health progression.

## How to Improve HRV Over Time

| Strategy | How It Helps |
|----------|-------------|
| Regular aerobic exercise | Strengthens cardiovascular function, directly boosts HRV |
| Stress management (meditation, breathing) | Reduces sympathetic activation, enhances HRV |
| Consistent sleep schedule | Stabilizes autonomic rhythms |
| Whole-food nutrition | Reduces systemic inflammation |
| Limiting alcohol | Even moderate alcohol significantly suppresses HRV for 24–48h |
| Quitting smoking | Improves cardiovascular health and HRV over weeks |
| Outdoor time and sunlight | Regulates cortisol and melatonin, promotes parasympathetic recovery |

::: tip
Consult a healthcare professional before making major lifestyle changes, especially if you have existing health conditions.
:::
